\subsubsection{Snowflake in the ARYZTA Data Ecosystem}

To address its data integration and analytics requirements, ARYZTA adopted \textbf{Snowflake} as its central cloud data platform. Snowflake's architecture---which decouples storage from compute, supports multi-cluster warehousing, and provides native data sharing capabilities---made it particularly well-suited to ARYZTA's needs for scalable, cost-efficient data processing across multiple business domains.

Within the ARYZTA data ecosystem, Snowflake serves as the integration layer for data sourced from ERP systems, logistics platforms, and commercial applications. Data pipelines load, transform, and expose curated datasets that feed downstream BI and reporting tools used by finance, operations, and supply chain teams. The Snowflake environment is therefore a mission-critical component of ARYZTA's operational infrastructure, and its stability directly affects the quality and availability of business-critical reporting.

The platform is structured around three separate Snowflake accounts---Development, User Acceptance Testing, and Production---each intended to correspond to a distinct stage in the software delivery lifecycle. As described in subsequent sections, the absence of governed deployment practices has compromised the integrity of this multi-environment architecture.

\subsection{Context and Motivation}
\label{subsec:context-motivation}

The ARYZTA data platform was managed prior to IBM involvement by a third-party consultancy. During that period, the platform underwent extensive development, but without adherence to structured DevOps or DataOps practices. The absence of a version-controlled deployment workflow, combined with a culture of direct production modifications, resulted in a state of progressive environment drift that had accumulated over a period of several years.

When IBM assumed responsibility for the platform, the team was confronted with a data infrastructure in which the three Snowflake environments---\texttt{DEV}, \texttt{UAT}, and \texttt{PROD}---had diverged to such a degree that \texttt{PROD} could no longer be considered a faithful replica of any formally documented state. Tables, stored procedures, tasks, streams, and views existed in production that had no counterpart in the lower environments, and vice versa. Schema structures were inconsistent across environments, and pipeline configurations had been individually tuned in production without those changes being propagated back to \texttt{DEV} or \texttt{UAT}.

This situation created a self-reinforcing problem: because \texttt{DEV} and \texttt{UAT} were not aligned with \texttt{PROD}, testing new features or changes in lower environments provided no reliable guarantee of behavior in production. As a result, engineers were routinely compelled to bypass the lower environments and apply changes directly to \texttt{PROD}---a practice that further exacerbated the drift and increased the probability of production incidents.

The business motivation for addressing these issues is straightforward: a stable, governed data platform is a prerequisite for reliable analytics, confident product development, and sustainable platform evolution. The implementation of environment synchronization and CI/CD automation is therefore not merely a technical improvement but a foundational investment in ARYZTA's data engineering capability.

\subsection{Risks and Challenges}
\label{subsec:risks-challenges}

The combination of environment drift and ungoverned deployment practices exposes ARYZTA's data platform to a range of technical and operational risks. These may be categorized along two dimensions: deployment risk and maintenance risk.

\paragraph{Deployment Risk.} The absence of a validated lower-environment proxy for production means that the consequences of any given deployment are difficult to anticipate. A change deployed directly to \texttt{PROD} without prior testing may introduce schema conflicts, break downstream transformations, or corrupt dependent pipelines. Given the business-critical nature of the ARYZTA data platform, such incidents can directly affect operational reporting and supply chain visibility.

\paragraph{Maintenance and Auditability Risk.} Without version control, there is no definitive record of what changes were applied to each environment, by whom, and when. This makes incident debugging significantly more difficult, as engineers cannot reliably reconstruct the sequence of changes that preceded a failure. It also complicates the onboarding of new team members, who must infer the current state of the platform through direct environment inspection rather than consulting a versioned codebase.

\paragraph{Governance Risk.} The lack of \gls{rbac}-enforced deployment workflows means that any engineer with Snowflake account access can modify production objects directly. This represents a significant governance exposure, particularly in the context of data compliance requirements.

\paragraph{Scalability Risk.} As the platform evolves and the number of Snowflake objects grows, the complexity of maintaining consistency between environments through manual means increases non-linearly. Without automation, the technical debt associated with environment misalignment will continue to accumulate.

\subsection{Specific Problem Addressed}
\label{subsec:specific-problem}

Based on the preliminary assessment conducted at the outset of the internship, the problem can be understood as the conjunction of two distinct but mutually reinforcing gaps. The first is a \textbf{measurement gap}: the team had no reliable, safe, and repeatable way to determine \emph{how far} the three environments had actually diverged, since the only authoritative record of each environment's state was the environment itself, and inspecting production for comparison purposes is inherently risky. The second is a \textbf{governance gap}: even where divergence was understood, there existed no version-controlled, automated, and auditable mechanism to correct it and to keep the environments aligned thereafter. The first gap makes the problem invisible; the second makes it irreversible. The specific technical problem addressed in this project can therefore be formally defined as follows:

\begin{tcolorbox}[notebox, title={Problem Statement}]
ARYZTA's Snowflake data platform operates across three environments---\texttt{DEV}, \texttt{UAT}, and \texttt{PROD}---that have diverged significantly in schema structure, object definitions, and pipeline configuration after years of ungoverned, manual, and undocumented deployments. The team possesses neither an instrument to \emph{measure} this divergence without endangering production, nor a version-controlled, automated process to \emph{remediate} it and prevent its recurrence. As a consequence, the lower environments cannot be trusted as proxies for production, changes are applied directly to \texttt{PROD}, and the risk of data-platform incidents grows continuously. The project must therefore deliver, jointly, a safe diagnostic capability and a governed remediation capability.
\end{tcolorbox}

This problem encompasses the following specific technical dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Absence of a Safe Diagnostic Instrument}: No tooling existed to compare the environments object by object. Any comparison performed by hand risked issuing write operations against production, so the true extent of the drift was simply unknown.
  \item \textbf{Environment Misalignment}: \texttt{DEV} and \texttt{UAT} do not accurately reflect the \texttt{PROD} state, so lower-environment testing offers no reliable guarantee of production behaviour. The divergence affects even the platform's operational backbone---the main task DAG, whose processing alone spans fourteen databases.
  \item \textbf{Missing Snowflake Objects}: Tables, stored procedures, tasks, streams, and other objects present in \texttt{PROD} have no counterpart in \texttt{DEV} and/or \texttt{UAT}, making full end-to-end pipeline testing in the lower environments structurally impossible.
  \item \textbf{Inconsistent Object Definitions}: Where objects do exist on both sides, their definitions diverge---column data types, precision, nullability, default values, and code (DDL) bodies---introducing transformation failures that surface only at scale in production.
  \item \textbf{Absent Deployment Automation and Version Control}: No central repository tracks the authoritative state of the Snowflake codebase, and no automated pipeline validates, promotes, and verifies changes across environments. The current platform state is consequently neither reproducible nor auditable.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Agile Delivery Framework}
\label{subsec:agile}

The project was delivered using an Agile, ServiceNow-driven workflow that combines the cadence of Scrum with the continuous, ticket-based flow of Kanban~\citep{schwaber2020scrum}. Every unit of work originates as a ServiceNow ticket and advances through a well-defined lifecycle---backlog triage and refinement, requirements and data analysis, development, testing and validation, delivery, and closure---with a continuous feedback loop that returns new requirements to the backlog. This flow is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:agile-kanban}. The model suited a project whose scope could only be determined progressively, as the audit revealed the true extent of the environment drift.

\subsubsection{Sprint Structure}

The team operated on \textbf{one-week sprints}, with retrospectives folded in at a monthly cadence rather than every sprint, drawing on the Scrum framework's standard ceremonies adapted to the team's delivery rhythm:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Monday} --- sprint planning: backlog review, story estimation, and a written commitment to the week's deliverables.
    \item \textbf{Tuesday to Friday} --- execution: development, testing, troubleshooting, and the documentation that accompanies each story.
    \item \textbf{Friday morning} --- sprint review: live demonstration of finished work to stakeholders, with feedback captured in writing.
    \item \textbf{Last Friday afternoon of each month} --- retrospective: a structured team conversation about what went well, what stalled, and what to change in the next four-sprint window.
\end{itemize}

Daily stand-ups (15 minutes) maintained team synchronization and surfaced blockers quickly.

\subsubsection{Work Categorization}

Sprint work was typically distributed across three categories:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Enhancements:} implementing new business logic, adding data-quality checks, and optimizing transformations (planned work).
    \item \textbf{Investigations:} root-cause analysis for data-quality issues, performance bottlenecks, or pipeline failures (reactive work).
    \item \textbf{Documentation:} runbooks, architecture diagrams, data lineage, and operational procedures (knowledge management).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Project Timeline}
\label{subsec:gantt}

The internship spanned approximately four and a half months, from \textbf{16 February 2026} to \textbf{1 July 2026}, and was organised into six sequential phases. Each phase corresponds to a distinct set of deliverables and outcomes, summarised below and visualised in the Gantt chart of Figure~\ref{fig:gantt}.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Onboarding and Environment Discovery} (Weeks 1--2): Access provisioning and familiarisation with the Snowflake platform, the team's tooling, and the ARYZTA project context.
  \item \textbf{Environment Audit and Discrepancy Analysis} (Weeks 3--5): Development of the read-only comparison tool and a systematic audit of the \texttt{DEV}, \texttt{UAT}, and \texttt{PROD} environments, with all identified discrepancies documented.
  \item \textbf{Root Cause Analysis and Strategy Design} (Weeks 6--8): Investigation of the origins of the environment drift and design of the synchronisation strategy and CI/CD architecture, including tooling selection.
  \item \textbf{CI/CD Pipeline Implementation} (Weeks 9--14): Establishment of the GitHub repository and branching model and development of the GitHub Actions workflow, with automated validation and environment-scoped deployment.
  \item \textbf{Environment Synchronization Execution} (Weeks 15--17): Provisioning of the Secure Data Shares and the synchronisation procedure, and phased realignment of \texttt{DEV} and \texttt{UAT} with \texttt{PROD}.
  \item \textbf{Testing, Documentation, and Handover} (Weeks 18--20): End-to-end testing of the pipeline, production of the technical documentation, and knowledge transfer to the client and delivery teams.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Chapter Conclusion}

This chapter has established the organizational and technical foundation upon which the remainder of this report is built. The host organization---IBM Client Innovation Center (CIC) Morocco, and specifically its Data \& Technology Transformation practice---delivers the Snowflake data-platform engagement for the client, ARYZTA. ARYZTA operates a mission-critical Snowflake data platform that has suffered from years of ungoverned deployment practices, resulting in a state of environment drift that constrains the engineering team's ability to develop and deliver platform enhancements safely.

The project objectives are clearly defined: to audit and quantify the environment discrepancies through a safe diagnostic instrument, to synchronize the lower environments with production, to implement a CI/CD pipeline that enforces version-controlled deployments, and to establish a governance framework that will prevent a recurrence of the current situation. The Agile delivery framework, supported by ServiceNow and GitHub, provides the structured delivery model within which these objectives are pursued.

The following chapter presents a rigorous analysis of the current state of the platform, including a detailed characterization of the discrepancies observed, their operational impact, and the factors that gave rise to them.
